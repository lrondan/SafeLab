Risk Identification

Chemical hazards

Chemical risks in a laboratory are associated with the handling, storage and disposal of chemical substances that may represent a danger to human health, the environment or the laboratory infrastructure. These risks can manifest themselves in various forms, such as toxicity, flammability, reactivity, corrosiveness or explosiveness, depending on the properties of the chemicals used.

Chemical hazards identification

  1. Toxic Substances: Chemicals that can cause harm to health through inhalation, ingestion or skin contact (e.g. heavy metals, organic solvents, cyanides).
  2. Flammable Substances: Compounds that can easily ignite in the presence of an ignition source (e.g. alcohol, acetone, ether).
  3. Corrosive Substances: Products that can cause severe damage to living tissue or materials (for example, strong acids such as sulfuric acid or bases such as sodium hydroxide).
  4. Reactive Substances: Compounds that can react violently with other chemicals, water or air (e.g., peroxides, metallic sodium).
  5. Explosive Substances: Chemicals that can release large amounts of energy in the form of an explosion (e.g. nitrates, perchlorates).
  6. Hazardous Gases and Vapors: Toxic or asphyxiating gases that can accumulate in closed spaces (e.g., chlorine, ammonia, carbon monoxide).

Physical hazards

Physical hazards in a laboratory are associated with environmental factors, equipment and conditions that can cause physical harm to personnel, such as injuries, burns, cuts or radiation exposure. These risks can arise from the use of machinery, tools, extreme temperatures, noise, inadequate lighting or radiation. Key aspects related to physical hazards are detailed below:

Physical hazards identification

  1. Mechanical Hazards: Risks associated with the use of machinery and equipment, such as moving parts, sharp edges or pinch points (e.g., centrifuges, mixers, cutting tools).
  2. Thermal Hazards: Dangers related to extreme temperatures, including burns from hot surfaces or cold burns from cryogenic materials (e.g., Bunsen burners, liquid nitrogen).
  3. Electrical Hazards: Risks of electric shock or fire due to faulty wiring, damaged equipment or improper use of electrical devices (e.g., power strips, laboratory instruments).
  4. Radiation Hazards: Exposure to ionizing radiation (e.g., X-rays, radioactive materials) or non-ionizing radiation (e.g., UV light) that can cause health issues (e.g., cancer, skin damage).
  5. Noise Hazards: Prolonged exposure to high noise levels from equipment can lead to hearing loss or stress (e.g., centrifuges, autoclaves).
  6. Ergonomic Hazards: Risks associated with poor workstation design or repetitive tasks that can lead to musculoskeletal disorders (e.g., pipetting, microscope use).
